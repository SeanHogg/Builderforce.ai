# Everything an Agentic Employee Can Do on BuilderForce

On most platforms an "AI agent" is a chat box with some tools bolted on. On BuilderForce an **agentic employee (EMP)** is a member of your workforce — it has an identity, a skill set, a personality, an owner, a place on the board, and a paper trail. This is the full picture of what one can do.

![The lifecycle of an agentic employee: hired from the marketplace, skilled with knowledge and personality, assigned on the same board as people, executing across surfaces, governed by approval gates, and trained into your own Evermind model](/blog/agentic-employee.svg)

## It's hired, not configured

An EMP starts as a hire. Browse the Workforce/marketplace, pick a specialist, and hire it — it appears on your roster alongside your people. You can also stand up your own: define a need in plain language, from a process chart, from a dataset, or from diagnostic findings, and the platform compiles it into an agent. Unhiring is a soft delete, so history is never lost.

## It carries skills, knowledge, and a personality

An EMP is more than a model. It is:

- **Skills** — reusable capabilities assigned to it project-wide or per agent.
- **Knowledge** — SOPs, processes, and documents it can read and act on, with versioning and read-acknowledgement for compliance.
- **A personality** — a psychometric persona (a trait vector) compiled into prompt directives and execution parameters, so a "cautious reviewer" and a "fast prototyper" genuinely behave differently.
- **A limbic layer** — an affective dynamics model that rides the personality, so behavior has setpoints *and* dynamics.

## It works on the same board as your people

An EMP is an assignee. Drag a ticket into its lane, or assign it directly — humans and agents share one Kanban board with swimlanes per status or per agent, plus table, calendar, and Gantt views. When an EMP is handed a task it assesses scope: if the work is really an epic, it decomposes it into child tasks and fans them out to other members — human or agent.

## It executes wherever the work lives

The same EMP can run on the surface that fits the job:

- **In the IDE / Designer** — building software in an in-browser Node runtime.
- **On-prem (desktop)** — a hosted runner on your own machine, for work that must stay local.
- **In the cloud** — durable or container-backed runners that survive long jobs.
- **As a workflow step** — embedded into a visual IPAAS-style workflow you draw.

It codes, opens pull requests, runs builds and tests, and reports back — and its work appears on the observability timeline automatically.

## It asks when it should

An EMP is human-in-the-loop by design. Write operations can pass through approval gates; a blocked agent can ask a human a question (over Slack or email) and pause durably until you answer. You review and approve actions — including from inside VS Code — without leaving your editor. Cost is forecast before execution, not discovered after.

## It's governed and observable

Every action an EMP takes is on the record: an execution timeline, per-tool emissions, DORA and member metrics, and a unified activity rollup. Policy packs can gate what agents may do; a governance auditor can review their pull requests. You always know what your agentic employees did, what it cost, and whether it was allowed.

## It can become *your* model

The ultimate EMP isn't a frozen frontier model — it's one you own. Train a custom model in-browser (WebGPU), benchmark it, and publish it as **Evermind**, a self-updating model governed by write-through cognition. Your specialists stop being prompts wrapped around someone else's model and start being a model that carries your knowledge and updates as you do.

## The point

Humans manage; agents do the work. An EMP gives you something to manage like an employee — hire it, skill it, give it a personality, assign it, supervise it, govern it, and grow it into a model of your own. That's the difference between a chatbot and a workforce.
