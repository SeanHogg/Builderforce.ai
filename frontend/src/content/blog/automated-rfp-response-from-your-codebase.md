# From Codebase to Costed Proposal: Automated RFP & RFQ Responses

Responding to a request for proposal is a strange kind of work: it's high-stakes, deadline-bound, and almost entirely a re-statement of things you already know. What can we build? How long will it take? What will it cost? Who staffs it? The answers live in your codebase and your team — but assembling them into a branded, defensible document eats days of senior time every single time.

Builderforce.ai answers an RFP from what you've already built.

## The proposal writes itself from what exists

The platform continuously analyzes each project's real capabilities. An RFP response reads from that analysis rather than from a blank template, and two agents author the result:

- A **CTO agent** frames the technical solution, the delivery phases, the risks, and the dependencies against the actual system.
- A **Product-Owner agent** frames the scope, the capability match, and the value story against the requester's ask.

What comes out is a complete proposal, not a skeleton: **cost and a P&L**, a **phased delivery Gantt**, a **risk register**, **dependencies**, and a **capability roster** matched line-by-line to what was requested.

## Co-branded, and grounded in reality

A proposal has to look like it was made for the reader. The document blends the **requester's brand palette and logo** with your own, so it arrives co-branded rather than generic — a self-contained artifact you can hand over or print to PDF.

And it has to be *true*. If the project's analysis is stale, the platform doesn't quietly answer from old data: **freshness-gated grounding** re-runs the deterministic system audits before generating, so the roster and the risks reflect the system as it is today, not as it was a month ago.

## Where it lives

RFP responses are a tab on the project itself — list and create from the Projects **RFP** view, then open `/projects/rfp/[id]` for the full detail: the capability roster, the P&L, the phase Gantt, the risks and dependencies, and the branded document to download. The proposal sits next to the work it describes, so it stays honest as the project evolves.

## Why it matters

Pre-sales is where a lot of engineering leadership time goes to die — reconstructing, by hand, facts the system already knows, under a clock. Turning the codebase into the proposal collapses that: the estimate is grounded in the real architecture, the cost rolls up from real numbers, and the roster is matched to genuine capability. You spend the saved days winning the next deal instead of formatting the last one.
